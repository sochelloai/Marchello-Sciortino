# Brand Voice Guide: Marchello Sciortino

This document serves as the private voice guide for Marchello Sciortino’s umbrella brand. It codifies the distinct patterns found in his raw writing samples and defines the boundaries of how his story, services, and advocacy are communicated.

---

## DO SAY: 12 Concrete Rules

### 1. Insert physical realities as casual, parenthetical context
* **Rule:** Drop details about physical environments, physical limitations, or equipment inside parentheses as matter-of-fact context, rather than framing them as a tragic focus.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 1 ("(the dude needs all the energy he's got...)") and Sample 3 ("(your boy’s dependent on a wheelchair…ahh what can be done?)").
* **Example:** "I like getting up early, but I try to hold out until mom is awake (lifting my weight is taxing on her body)."

### 2. Comment directly on the physical writing or typing process
* **Rule:** Call out the difficulty, speed, or fatigue of typing or formatting in real time as meta-commentary.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 1 ("Thanks for the vent, this took me 3 hours to write (typing is a nightmare.)").
* **Example:** "Let's get right into it, even though typing this out is a absolute nightmare."

### 3. Use self-deprecating irony to describe difficult feelings
* **Rule:** Defuse heavy topics (like dependency or fatigue) by using self-directed labels such as "mooch," "addict," or "psychotic."
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 1 ("like a disabled mooch, an energy draining soul sucker?") and Sample 2 ("Hello, my name is Marchello Sciortino and I’m an addict...").
* **Example:** "How do you avoid feeling like a disabled mooch when you have to wait on everyone else?"

### 4. Use ellipses (`…` or `...`) to separate thoughts instead of formal punctuation
* **Rule:** Use ellipses to indicate natural pauses, trailing thoughts, or sudden pivots in direction to mimic spoken rhythm.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 1 ("woes me… except the concern") and Sample 2 ("because I'm tired…because I'm exhausted…because I'm confused…").
* **Example:** "This is old, yet new…past, yet present…wait, what are you trying to say?"

### 5. Write incomplete sentence fragments to mirror immediate thoughts
* **Rule:** Skip formal sentence structures, subjects, or verbs to keep the rhythm conversational and direct.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 2 ("Spoiled? No doubt") and Sample 3 ("3rd reschedule (haha, 4th times a charm), kinda felt like...").
* **Example:** "Practice was great, but the game? Refused to leave my parents' side."

### 6. Inject phonetic vocal fillers and laugh indicators
* **Rule:** Write out spoken cues like "ummm," "ah-hem," "c'mon," or "haha" to make the reader feel like they are listening to a live speaker.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 2 ("Ah-hem (clearing throat)", "Ummm NOOOO", "c'mon main") and Sample 3 ("haha, 4th times a charm").
* **Example:** "Ummm, dad, that’s a pretty decent sized bullseye rash on your back."

### 7. Address the reader directly to check if they are following
* **Rule:** Interrupt the narrative flow to ask questions or check in with the audience in the second person.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 2 ("(You following, cause I’m not.)" and "You know how I mentioned I was shy?").
* **Example:** "Hold on, this part is important—are you following, or did I lose you?"

### 8. Juxtapose serious struggle and humor without transition words
* **Rule:** Place raw descriptions of pain or limitation directly next to jokes, pop-culture quotes, or tangents without warning or comforting transitions.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 2 (jumping from rolling on the floor in pain to: "(Hello! Took a little 3-day break…where were we?)").
* **Example:** "I would roll on the floor crying from muscle cramps. Anyway, fifth grade was pretty cool."

### 9. Include parenthetical jokes inside parentheses
* **Rule:** Double-layer your thoughts by inserting jokes, corrections, or self-dialogue within existing parenthetical statements.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 1 ("(I always have my phone near... *A couple times I forced myself...)").
* **Example:** "I always keep my phone nearby (usually on my bed, though sometimes it slips out of reach—yikes)."

### 10. Refer to yourself in the third person using colloquial nicknames
* **Rule:** Use informal third-person nicknames like "your boy" or "a drunk kid" to narrate your own actions and keep the tone light.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 3 ("your boy’s dependent on a wheelchair") and Sample 2 ("it’s not the best idea for a “drunk” kid to wear drunk effect goggles").
* **Example:** "They wanted me to lift my legs, but at the last second, I remembered your boy can't walk."

### 11. Spell words phonetically to match casual speech
* **Rule:** Spell words phonetically to capture regional dialect or casual speech (e.g., using "main" instead of "man" or "w/" instead of "with").
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 2 ("c'mon main") and Sample 2 ("any obstacle in my path…wow").
* **Example:** "C'mon main, we both know it's not that simple."

### 12. Ask self-reflective questions and answer them immediately
* **Rule:** Create internal dialogue by posing questions to yourself in the middle of a paragraph and writing out the direct reply.
* **Citation:** Derived from Sample 2 ("What am I writing about again??? Oh yeah!" and "Am I the sole owner of this vessel? Answer: No.").
* **Example:** "Should I have stayed in bed? Probably, but we went anyway."

## DON'T SAY: 12 Concrete Rules

### 1. Avoid generic inspirational platitudes
* **Rule:** Never use standard motivational slogans like "winning despite the odds," "we all have challenges," or "we are all more similar than we think."
* **Citation:** Identified as generic elements in Sample 1 ("we all have challenges") and Sample 3 ("winning despite the odds!").
* **Example:** "We must strive to win despite the odds that life gives us."

### 2. Avoid bucket-list and action-hero jargon
* **Rule:** Do not use clichés like "checking boxes," "pushing the envelope," or "on the road to badassery."
* **Citation:** Identified as generic idioms in Sample 3 ("Box checked", "On the road to badassery").
* **Example:** "Let's check this box and push the envelope."

### 3. Avoid formal storytelling signposts
* **Rule:** Do not use phrases that explicitly announce a story is beginning or signal suspense.
* **Citation:** Identified as generic transitions in Sample 2 ("Here’s a good story:" and "Okay, so now that suspense has somewhat built…").
* **Example:** "Here is a very interesting story about what happened next."

### 4. Avoid objective, clinical, textbook-style descriptions of conditions
* **Rule:** Do not write clinical or medical definitions of symptoms or diseases; keep descriptions personal and narrative.
* **Citation:** Identified as generic explanation in Sample 2 ("A bullseye rash is usually a clear sign of early contraction of Lyme disease.").
* **Example:** "Friedrich's ataxia is characterized by progressive damage to the nervous system."

### 5. Avoid stacking exclamation points for manufactured excitement
* **Rule:** Do not use exclamation marks to inflate the energy of a sentence unless it is a deliberate parody.
* **Citation:** Identified as generic energy markers in Sample 3 ("Woah...that was amazing!" and "torrential downpour!").
* **Example:** "We were flying high up in the sky!"

### 6. Avoid standard transition phrases to recover from tangents
* **Rule:** Do not use typical phrases like "Anyway, back to my point" or "Back to what I was saying" to bridge topics.
* **Citation:** Identified as generic resets in Sample 2 ("Back to what I was saying!" and "Anyway, around third grade...").
* **Example:** "Anyway, back to what I was saying about the middle school gym class."

### 7. Avoid corporate buzzwords and marketing jargon
* **Rule:** Never use professional services clichés like "leverage," "synergy," "optimize," "value proposition," or "transformative solutions."
* **Citation:** Explicitly flagged as corporate voice errors in user instructions.
* **Example:** "We leverage optimized digital strategies to transform your business."

### 8. Avoid artificial sales pressure phrases
* **Rule:** Do not use transactional scarcity language like "buy now," "act fast," "limited-time offer," or "exclusive opportunity."
* **Citation:** Explicitly flagged as sales pressure errors in user instructions.
* **Example:** "Act fast and grab this exclusive opportunity today!"

### 9. Avoid self-glorifying or "unconquered hero" language
* **Rule:** Do not claim to have "conquered everything," "beaten every obstacle," or become "unstoppable."
* **Citation:** Explicitly flagged as self-glorification errors in user instructions.
* **Example:** "I have beaten every obstacle and built an unstoppable mind."

### 10. Avoid pity-inducing terminology when describing disability
* **Rule:** Do not use words like "wheelchair-bound," "confined," "victim," or "suffering from."
* **Citation:** Explicitly flagged as pity language in user instructions and Sample 1.
* **Example:** "For years I have been confined to a wheelchair, suffering from a muscle disease."

### 11. Avoid patronizing sympathy language
* **Rule:** Do not use phrases like "I feel sorry for you," "your life must be so hard," or "I don't know how you do it."
* **Citation:** Explicitly flagged as sympathy/pity language in user instructions.
* **Example:** "Your life is so hard, and I don't know how you do it every day."

### 12. Avoid empty mindset slogans
* **Rule:** Do not use oversimplified phrases like "mindset is everything," "never give up," "follow your dreams," or "winners never quit."
* **Citation:** Explicitly flagged as cliché errors in user instructions.
* **Example:** "If you just follow your dreams and remember that mindset is everything, you will win."
